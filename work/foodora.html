This project taught me that the foundation of good design is understanding and research. How can we design, if we don't know who or what we're designing for?

The Problem

The prompt of this assignment was to choose a food-delivery service and redesign a major feature, using reasoning and data from user research. We asked ourselves a few questions: How can we improve the user experience of Foodora? How can we use the design process to understand and empathize with the user, identify major functionalities, and create and iterate on a low-fidelity prototype?

The Process

üîç Research and Understanding our Users

We designed a survey and gathered data from over 70 reddit users, to understand specifically why people use food delivery apps and their motivations at each stage. We wanted to see what the general thought process was (does dish matter more? or restaurant?) as well as any pain points in the journey. The insights helped us define a few major use cases of Foodora that we could iterate on.

‚úèÔ∏è Task Analysis + Definition

The purpose of the task analysis was to clearly define primary functions of the system, examine user goals, as well as their means to achieve them and whether the system allows them to do so in an efficient manner. From the results of our research, we concluded on 3 different main tasks.
  • Account Creation,
  • Restaurant/food Selection
  • Checkout
We decided that browsing food/restaurant should be redesigned, as there were the most conflicts between the users preferences and how the interface works - as indicated by the research conducted.

We wanted to merge the two user flows into one. It seemed unintuitive that the search functionality was completely different for food and restaurant.

üëî Results: A Low-fidelity Prototype

We redesigned the user experience based on three main issues:
  1. Lack of flexibility and efficiency in browsing food and restaurants
  2. Lack of discriminability and high information access cost
  3. Information hierarchy in food cards did not conform to user preferences

To target the first issue, we designed an interface that allowed users to freely switch between browsing food and restaurants.

To reduce the information access cost to the checkout, we emphasized the total cost and time to delivery in the checkout hover card.

We also redesigned the foodcards so that it was obvious which items were required to complete the order and which weren't, as well as move required items to the top.

üëî User Testing and Observations

We designed a user test consisting of an introduction script, preliminary survey, the testing of three tasks using a think-aloud protocol, and a post-test interview. The three tasks were: finding a dish, finding a restaurant, and customizing and adding a dish to the cart.

We tested on students that were not Foodora users but also had some familiarity with online food ordering.

  • users did not understand the food description icons
