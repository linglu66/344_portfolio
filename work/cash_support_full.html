the story

It was very exciting to be able to own the design of a feature as an intern - working with a defined design system and managing expectations between PM, engineering and design was definitely an interesting challenge.

Although this feature ended up being moved to further down the roadmap, it was a great learning experience nonetheless.

the challenge

Cash App’s mission is to increase economic empowerment and make finances easier - however as a quickly growing company, it can be difficult to provide a quality level of support.

In our support channel, few customers successfully find a solution using self-serve methods. How might we improve our self-service support such that content is more discoverable, and customers can easily find useful articles?

Defining the problem

How can we improve content discoverability?

Currently, our customers must navigate through a content tree with little guidance as to topics, direction or organization. This is not ideal as customers who are under distress and looking for solutions, may not be willing to bear the addition cognitive load required to navigate through our content (although a solution may just be a few clicks away).

Referencing Data

Validating the Story

Our data tells reveals a similar story: on entering our support page,
- 64% of customers create a case within 30 seconds.
- 75% of customers create a case under a minute

Almost a tenth of cases are created when the customers are repeatedly scrolling to the bottom, not reading the content and just trying to find a “contact support button”.

Evidently, we have a very small budget of customer attention to work with, justifying the need for a quick and low-effort interaction. Based on our findings, we decided to create a search system.
Metrics

Measuring Success

Our overall goal is to increase the effectiveness of our self-service support page and reduce the amount of cases that need to be handled by humans.
  • Metric: Reduce the average time to resolution
  • Metric: Increase the overall resolution rate
    • To evaluate the specifics of how well our search solution would work, we defined some secondary metrics.
  • Click behaviour, indicating successful search rankings
  • Dwell time on articles that search surfaces (is it a good match?)
  • Lowered case-creation rate of customers who initiate a search
    • Empathy practice

    With a better understanding of the user journey, I broke the solution down into a few components.

    Discoverability/Entry point: Where should support search live? Should it be front and center, or be a secondary function?

    Entering the query: Which information is most relevant to our user? How can we use information hierarchy to improve scalability and understanding of our search results?

    Iterating on designs

    Gathering Feedback

    I held a design critique and some informal user tests to get feedback on the first iteration. Some issues that surfaced:
    Issue 1

    Separation of historical searches

    It was confusing for the historical searches to be under the 'suggested' section. Although there is a differentiating icon, we agreed that it needed to be more distinct.
    Issue 2

    Inconsistency of article layouts

    The search experience was jarring when switching between many different article layouts. Users tend to search pretty quickly, so to rapidly change how we're showing articles might be confusing.
    Solution

    Final Solution

    Based on the feedback on the first iteration, I moved historical searches to its own section. The three different article layouts got replaced by a single, more unified layout. I also cleaned up the design to make it more skimmable.
    Reflections

    Digging into the details

    At first I thought search would have been a very straightforward feature, as it's a common design pattern - it wasn't until I mapped out the user flow that all the complexities surfaced.

    Next steps would be to test more thoroughly with real users, and examine the integration with the rest of our support content more closely. Categorized articles was also out of the scope this time around, but would be interesting the explore further.
